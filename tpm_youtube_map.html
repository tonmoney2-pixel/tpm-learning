TPM Scenario Questions
Scenario 1

Your engineer says the app "can't connect to the backend." A user in Tokyo is hitting a 503 error but users in New York are fine. What's your first question to the engineering team and why?

Ask whether the issue is DNS-related or routing-related — specifically whether the DNS record for that region is resolving correctly, and whether the server serving Tokyo requests is healthy. A regional failure often points to a specific server farm or CDN edge node being down, not the entire service.
Scenario 2

A stakeholder asks: "Why does our app take 3 seconds to load when I'm on hotel Wi-Fi in Singapore but loads instantly at home?" How do you explain this without jargon?

Explain that data travels physically through cables under the ocean. Hotel Wi-Fi adds extra hops between their device and the network, and if your servers are in North America, the round-trip distance for every packet adds real time. Slow Wi-Fi also means smaller bandwidth — like a garden hose vs a fire hose — so the same data just takes longer.

TPM Scenario Questions
Scenario 1

Your PM asks: "Can we just make our servers bigger so the app doesn't crash during our product launch?" What are the trade-offs and what would you recommend instead?

Making servers bigger is vertical scaling — it has a ceiling and requires downtime to resize. A better approach for a launch spike is horizontal scaling with auto-scaling groups: spin up more instances automatically when traffic spikes and wind them down after. This is more resilient, avoids a single point of failure, and only costs money during the spike.
Scenario 2

Engineering tells you that auto-scaling "kicked in" during last night's traffic spike but users still experienced slowness for 90 seconds. Why might that happen and what should you ask?

New instances take time to start up, pass health checks, and register with the load balancer — this warm-up period is the likely culprit. Ask: what is the current instance startup time? Can we use pre-warmed instances or scheduled scaling before the spike? Could we set a higher minimum instance count during the event window?

TPM Scenario Questions
Scenario 1

A designer wants to add real-time price updates to a product page (price changes every few seconds). An engineer says this is "a backend change, not frontend." Why is this statement incomplete and what should you clarify?

Real-time updates require both sides to change. The backend needs to expose a way to push or serve live data (e.g. websockets or polling endpoint), and the frontend needs to listen for those updates and re-render without a full page reload. Framing it as backend-only misses the client-side rendering work and the state management needed in the UI.
Scenario 2

A bug report says "the button works in Chrome but not Safari." What layer of the stack is most likely responsible, and how does that affect how you prioritize the fix?

This is almost certainly a frontend/client-side issue — browser rendering inconsistencies live in the presentation layer. Since it's isolated to one browser, it affects a specific portion of your user base (check your analytics for Safari share). Prioritize based on that percentage: if 20%+ of users are on Safari, escalate quickly. It likely only requires a CSS or JS compatibility fix, not a backend change.

Scenario 1

Your team is evaluating whether to use React or Vue for a new internal dashboard. The engineering lead says "it doesn't matter, they both do the same thing." How do you facilitate this decision as a TPM?

As a TPM, push for a decision framework rather than a debate. Key questions: What does your existing team know? (Hiring/onboarding cost matters.) What does the rest of your stack use? TPM Scenario Questions
Scenario 1

A third-party payment API your team depends on starts returning 429 errors. Users can't check out. What does 429 mean, and what questions do you ask engineering immediately?

429 means "Too Many Requests" — you've exceeded the rate limit the payment provider allows. Ask: What is our current rate limit with this provider? Are we caching responses where possible? Do we have exponential backoff implemented (retry logic that slows down as requests fail)? Can we temporarily queue requests rather than dropping them? And — can we call the provider to request a temporary rate limit increase?
Scenario 2

Product wants to add a "Share to Social" feature. Engineering says they'll use the platform's API. As a TPM, what API-related risks should you surface before sprint planning?

Key risks: (1) Rate limiting — how many share calls per hour/day are allowed? (2) API versioning — what happens when the platform deprecates an endpoint? (3) Auth/token management — do we need OAuth flows? (4) Terms of service — does the platform allow programmatic sharing? (5) Response time — if the API is slow, does it block our UI? Surface these before sprint planning so engineering can scope accordingly.

TPM Scenario Questions
Scenario 1

Your product is launching in Southeast Asia. Engineering says images load in 400ms for US users but 3.2 seconds for users in Thailand. What's the most likely cause and what's the fastest technical fix?

The images are being served from a server located in the US or Europe — physical distance is the bottleneck. The fastest fix is to put a CDN in front of your image assets. A CDN caches images at edge servers geographically close to Thailand (e.g. Singapore), so subsequent requests load from nearby instead of crossing the Pacific. This can reduce image load time by 60–80% with minimal engineering effort.
Scenario 2

The VP of Engineering says "CDNs are expensive, let's not add one." You're launching a media-heavy feature. How do you make the business case?

Frame it in user and business terms: slow load times increase bounce rate — studies show each second of delay reduces conversions by ~7%. For a media-heavy feature, your origin server also takes a hit on every image request without a CDN, meaning higher compute costs and potential failure under load. CDN costs are often lower than the compute savings from offloading traffic. Present the math: CDN cost vs. expected server savings + projected conversion impact.

TPM Scenario Questions
Scenario 1

After deploying a critical pricing fix, some users still see the old prices. Support is getting flooded. Engineering says "the database is updated." What's happening and how do you fix it?

The old prices are being served from cache — either the CDN, the application cache, or the browser cache. Even though the database is correct, stale cached responses are still being returned. The fix is a cache purge or cache invalidation targeted at the pricing endpoint or page. Ask engineering: which caching layers are in front of the pricing data? Can we purge only that content without clearing the entire cache? Going forward, implement short cache TTLs for pricing data or use cache-busting keys.
Scenario 2

A user complains your app "shows broken images on slow connections." How would you explain graceful degradation to a non-technical stakeholder, and what engineering approach solves it?

Graceful degradation means the app still works at reduced quality when conditions are bad — like a car that can still drive with a flat tyre. For images: use incremental loading (show a blurred low-res placeholder while the full image loads), and specify image dimensions in code so the layout doesn't jump. For very slow connections, detect network speed and serve compressed images automatically. The user experience degrades gracefully instead of breaking entirely.

TPM Scenario Questions
Scenario 1

Your SEO team says your React app's pages aren't being indexed by Google properly. Engineering says "our pages load fine." What's the underlying technical conflict and what's the resolution?

React apps are Client-Side Rendered (CSR) — the server sends a mostly empty HTML shell, and JavaScript in the browser builds the page. Some search engine crawlers don't wait for JavaScript to execute, so they see empty content. The fix is Server-Side Rendering (SSR) or Static Site Generation for SEO-critical pages: the server sends fully rendered HTML so crawlers can read the content immediately. Frameworks like Next.js handle this.
Scenario 2

An engineer proposes making the checkout process asynchronous so users don't wait for the payment confirmation. A stakeholder is worried this will confuse users. How do you mediate this?

Async processing means the user gets an immediate "we're processing your order" response while the payment is confirmed in the background — reducing wait time but adding communication complexity. The stakeholder concern is valid: users need clear feedback. The solution is a well-designed loading state + email/push confirmation when the async task completes. Ask engineering to ensure fault tolerance (what happens if payment fails after the user is told it succeeded?) and work with design on the UX communication.

TPM Scenario Questions
Scenario 1

Engineering is debating whether to use PostgreSQL or MongoDB for a new user profile feature. As a TPM, what questions do you ask to help them decide?

Ask: (1) Is the data structure fixed or variable? (User profiles with flexible fields → MongoDB. Rigid structured data → PostgreSQL.) (2) Will we need complex queries across multiple data types? (SQL handles joins better.) (3) What does the rest of our stack use? (Consistency reduces operational complexity.) (4) What are the read/write patterns? (High write volume, document-oriented → NoSQL often wins.) (5) Do we need ACID transactions? (SQL is stronger here for financial or order data.)
Scenario 2

A stakeholder asks: "Why can't we just store everything in one big spreadsheet?" How do you explain why databases exist and why structure matters?

A spreadsheet works for hundreds of rows but breaks at scale — no enforced rules on data types, no efficient searching, no concurrent users, and no relationships between data sets. A database enforces structure (so no one accidentally puts text in a number field), allows thousands of users to read/write simultaneously without conflicts, handles millions of rows efficiently through indexing, and creates links between related data (e.g. a user → their orders → each order's products) without duplicating everything.

TPM Scenario Questions
Scenario 1

Product wants to add a "user tags" feature that lets users add unlimited custom labels to their profile. Engineering says this will require a schema change. Why, and what should you understand about the implications?

Currently the schema likely has a fixed set of columns for user data. "Unlimited custom tags" is a one-to-many relationship — one user, many tags — which needs a separate Tags table linked by user ID. Schema migrations on live databases carry risk: they can lock tables, require downtime, or cause performance issues at scale. As a TPM, ask: what is the migration plan? Will it require downtime? How large is the users table today? And — are we planning for future tag filtering/search, which changes the indexing requirements?

TPM Scenario Questions
Scenario 1

Your analytics team says a dashboard query is taking 45 seconds to load. Engineering says "it's just a slow query." What follow-up questions show you understand the problem at a technical level?

Ask: (1) Is the query doing a full table scan? (Missing index.) (2) How large is the table it's querying? (Millions of rows without indexes = slow.) (3) Is this query running in real-time or could we pre-compute it? (Move to a reporting/analytics replica or a data warehouse.) (4) Is this query being cached anywhere? (Dashboards don't need real-time data — a 5-min cache could eliminate the problem.) (5) Can we add an index on the columns being filtered? These questions show you understand that slow queries are usually an indexing, caching, or architecture problem, not just "code that runs slow."

TPM Scenario Questions
Scenario 1

Engineering proposes breaking your monolith into microservices "to be more scalable." The team is 4 engineers. What are the real trade-offs and how do you facilitate the right decision?

Microservices add significant operational complexity — each service needs its own deployment pipeline, monitoring, and network communication layer. For a 4-person team, that overhead often costs more than it saves. Push back with: What specific scaling problem are we solving today? (If none exists yet, it's premature optimization.) A well-structured monolith is easier to develop, test, and debug at this scale. Consider a modular monolith as a middle ground — clean separation of concerns without the distributed systems overhead. Revisit microservices when a specific service needs to scale independently.
Scenario 2

One of your microservices (the payment service) is down. How do you think through the blast radius of this failure and what does it tell you about your architecture?

Map which user-facing flows depend on the payment service: checkout, subscription renewal, refunds. Those are down. Now ask: did the failure cascade? Did other services try to call payment and fail loudly (crashing their own processes) or fail gracefully (returning an error and continuing)? Good microservice architecture uses circuit breakers to prevent cascading failures. This incident is a chance to audit: which services are tightly coupled to payment? Where do we lack graceful degradation? Update your incident runbook and consider adding fallback behaviour.

Scenario 1

Your app is launching on Product Hunt tomorrow. Engineering says "we can handle it." How do you validate that claim and what do you put in place as a TPM?

4.3 Message Queues & Email Outage Scenario ▼
Microservices and Message Queues — Explained
Covers: How queues work, polling, solving email outage scenarios · ~20 min
Queue Basics Watch
HTTP vs AMQP: Why Message Queueing Between Microservices?
Covers: Async communication, queue-based fault isolation, decoupling · ~15 min
Async Queues Watch
TPM Scenario Questions
Scenario 1

Your transactional email provider goes down for 2 hours. Users aren't receiving password reset emails. How does a message queue help here, and what would you have needed to implement beforehand?

Without a queue, a failed email send is a lost email — the request was made, the provider was down, it's gone. With a queue, the email request is placed into a durable queue. A worker polls the queue and tries to send; if the provider is down, the message stays in the queue and retries with backoff. When the provider recovers, all queued emails are sent automatically. You'd need to have built this queue-based architecture in advance — it's not something you can add during an incident. This is a good argument for building resilient email infrastructure from the start.

5.1 Phase 1: Planning ▼
What is SDLC? LifeCycle Explained in 5 Minutes — Intellipaat
Covers: Planning phase, requirements, user stories, 4 phases of planning · ~5 min
Planning Phase Watch
TPM Scenario Questions
Scenario 1

A product manager writes the user story: "As a user, I want a fast checkout." An engineer says this isn't useful. Why, and how would you rewrite it?

"Fast" is unmeasurable and provides no acceptance criteria. A better version: "As a returning user, I want to complete checkout in under 3 clicks so that I can purchase without re-entering saved payment details." This gives the engineer a user goal, a measurable success condition, and a reason why. Acceptance criteria would then specify: saved card auto-populates, shipping address pre-filled, single confirmation screen.
Scenario 2

Halfway through sprint planning, engineering discovers a feature will require 3x more work than estimated because of a hidden dependency. What's the TPM's role here?

Your job is to facilitate a rapid decision without derailing the sprint. Options: (1) Scope the feature down to fit the existing estimate — what's the MVP that still delivers value? (2) Move the full feature to the next sprint and replace it with something already estimated. (3) Accept the overrun if the feature is critical, and adjust scope elsewhere. Document the dependency as a known risk for future planning. Ask: was this dependency discoverable earlier? If yes, fix the requirement process.

Scenario 1

An engineer pushes directly to the main branch (skipping a PR review) to "save time." The change breaks production. How do you handle this as a TPM and what process do you put in place?

Address the immediate incident first: rollback the commit, restore production, communicate status to stakeholders. 5.4 Phase 4: Testing ▼
Software Development Life Cycle: Explained
Covers: Manual QA, test suites & cases, unit tests, integration tests · ~16 min
QA & Testing Watch
TPM Scenario Questions
Scenario 1

Engineering says they have 80% test coverage. A stakeholder says "great, we're nearly bug-free." How do you correct this misunderstanding?

Test coverage measures how much code is executed by tests — not whether the tests are testing the right things. 80% coverage still means 20% of the code has no tests at all, and the 80% might be testing trivial paths rather than critical edge cases. A checkout flow with 80% coverage could still fail on a specific card type or an unusual address format. The better question is: do we have tests for our most critical user journeys and the failure modes most likely to cause a production incident?
Scenario 2

QA finds a critical bug two days before launch. Engineering says fixing it will take 3 days. As a TPM, what are your options and how do you decide?

Assess severity and impact: How many users does this affect? Is it a blocker (feature doesn't work) or a degradation (works but poorly)? Options: (1) Delay launch by 3 days — appropriate if the bug affects a core flow. (2) Launch with the bug if it's in a non-critical flow, and fix in a follow-up patch. (3) Scope the launch — exclude the affected feature with a feature flag, launch the rest. Always communicate clearly upward: "Here's the trade-off, here's my recommendation." Avoid "we'll just test more" — that's not a plan.

TPM Scenario Questions
Scenario 1

Product wants to ship a new checkout redesign to all users simultaneously. Engineering proposes a feature flag rollout instead. How do you explain the benefit to a non-technical stakeholder?

A feature flag lets you turn the new checkout on for 5% of users first — real users, real traffic, but controlled exposure. If something breaks, you flip the flag off in seconds rather than rolling back a full deployment (which takes time and carries risk). You can also roll out to internal users first, then your beta group, then everyone — collecting data at each stage. It separates shipping the code (which happens quietly) from releasing the feature to users (which you control). It's a much safer way to ship something as critical as checkout.
Scenario 2

Your CI/CD pipeline is taking 45 minutes to run. Engineers are merging code less frequently because it's too slow. What are the risks and what should you push engineering to investigate?

A slow pipeline is a culture problem disguised as a technical one: teams that batch up changes to "save pipeline runs" accumulate bigger, riskier deployments. Ask engineering to profile the pipeline: which step takes the longest? (Usually tests or builds.) Can tests be parallelised? Can we cache build dependencies? Can we run only the tests affected by changed files rather than the full suite? Target is under 10 minutes for most teams. Each minute shaved off the pipeline directly improves how often engineers can safely ship.
5.6 Phase 6: Maintenance (Telemetry & Monitoring) ▼
The Secret Life of Software Development — SDLC & Monitoring
Covers: Telemetry, logs & severity levels, metrics, alerting, dashboards · ~22 min
Monitoring Watch
TPM Scenario Questions
Scenario 1

A major bug was live in production for 6 hours before anyone noticed. Engineering says "we have monitoring." What went wrong and what should you add?

Having monitoring and having effective alerting are different things. Common failure modes: (1) Alerts exist but thresholds are set too high — error rate alert at 50% when the real impact starts at 5%. (2) Alert fatigue — so many low-priority alerts that engineers ignore them. (3) The right metric wasn't tracked — error rates looked fine but conversion rates dropped (a business metric, not a technical one). Push for: tighter alert thresholds on critical paths, synthetic monitoring (automated tests that simulate real user flows every minute), and adding business metrics like checkout completion rate to your alerting dashboard.
Scenario 2

The VP asks: "Is the new feature we shipped performing well?" Engineering shows a chart of server response time. Is that enough? What else should you be tracking?

Server response time is a technical metric — it tells you the server is fast, not that the feature is succeeding. A complete picture includes: (1) Adoption — what % of users are using the new feature? (2) Completion rate — do they finish the flow or drop off? (3) Error rate — what % of attempts fail? (4) Business impact — has the KPI this feature was built to move (revenue, signups, retention) changed? (5) Support ticket volume — are users confused? As a TPM, your job is to connect the technical metrics to the business metrics in that VP conversation.
